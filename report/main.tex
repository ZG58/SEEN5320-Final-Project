\begin{abstract}
This project builds a machine-learning workflow for a vacuum pressure swing adsorption (VPSA) CO$_2$ capture process using raw gPROMS simulation samples. The source file \path{data/raw_data.mat} contains 1708 successful simulations from an original 2000-point Latin hypercube design. Six operating variables are mapped to four scalar performance indicators: CO$_2$ recovery, CO$_2$ purity, productivity, and specific energy. Eight surrogate model families are tuned and compared on both global test accuracy and Pareto-focused accuracy. The best model, an RBF support vector regression surrogate, is then coupled with an in-house NSGA-II optimizer to generate surrogate Pareto fronts for purity-recovery and productivity-energy optimization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background and Objective}
VPSA is attractive for post-combustion CO$_2$ capture because pressure-driven regeneration can be operated cyclically with relatively simple equipment. The reference study by Wang et al. \cite{wang2022comparative} developed a 3-bed 7-step VPSA process for dry flue gas separation and used artificial neural-network surrogates to accelerate multi-objective optimization. The same project logic is followed here: detailed gPROMS simulations are treated as expensive samples, machine-learning models learn the input-output relationship, and a fast surrogate is used to search for improved operating conditions.

The project objective is to create a reproducible data-to-optimization pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item convert the MATLAB simulation file into a clean CSV table;
    \item train and tune several regression surrogates for the four VPSA KPIs;
    \item evaluate accuracy globally and near empirical Pareto regions;
    \item optimize the best surrogate with NSGA-II;
    \item summarize the workflow and results in this report and the accompanying slides.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Basis}
Wang et al. \cite{wang2022comparative} solved two VPSA multi-objective problems: maximizing purity and recovery, and maximizing productivity while minimizing energy under purity and recovery constraints. Their detailed process used silica gel adsorbent, a 15/85 mol\% CO$_2$/N$_2$ feed, and six major decision variables: feed flowrate, replacement flowrate, vacuum flowrate, replacement time, equalization-depressurization time, and pressurization time. Their work motivates the same surrogate-assisted structure used here.

\section{Surrogate Modeling}
The training script compares eight model families: ridge regression, polynomial ridge regression, RBF kernel ridge regression, RBF SVR, random forest, extra trees, histogram gradient boosting, and multilayer perceptron regression. The data are split into 80\% training and 20\% testing with random seed 42. Hyperparameters are selected by 3-fold cross-validation on the training set. Energy is modeled in log space to reduce scale imbalance, then transformed back to kJ kgCO$_2^{-1}$ for reporting.

Models are ranked by
\[
S = 0.6 E_{\mathrm{Pareto}} + 0.4 E_{\mathrm{global}},
\]
where $E$ is the mean normalized RMSE across recovery, purity, productivity, and log energy. This score emphasizes accuracy near the regions that matter most for optimization.

\section{Surrogate-Assisted Optimization}
The selected RBF SVR is optimized with an in-house NSGA-II implementation. Candidate solutions are represented as normalized decision variables and decoded to the physical bounds in Table \ref{tab:bounds}. The optimizer uses simulated binary crossover, polynomial mutation, nondominated sorting, and crowding distance selection.

Two problems are solved:
\[
\max\; (\mathrm{purity},\mathrm{recovery})
\]
and
\[
\max\; \mathrm{productivity}, \quad \min\; \mathrm{energy},
\quad \mathrm{subject\ to}\quad \mathrm{purity}\ge 0.70,\; \mathrm{recovery}\ge 0.90.
\]
Both runs use population size 160 and 250 generations.

\section{Discussion}
The purity-recovery front shows a strong compromise. The maximum-purity candidate reaches the observed upper purity bound of 0.918, but recovery falls to 0.349 and energy rises to about 2897 kJ kgCO$_2^{-1}$. The maximum-recovery candidate reaches 0.935 recovery with purity of 0.719, productivity of 0.358 mol h$^{-1}$ kg$^{-1}$, and energy of 1139 kJ kgCO$_2^{-1}$. This behavior matches the physical intuition from cyclic adsorption: pushing the bed toward higher enrichment can reduce capture recovery, while aggressive recovery can dilute product purity.

The constrained productivity-energy problem gives a narrower front. All nondominated candidates satisfy the purity and recovery constraints, and the optimizer concentrates near recovery of 0.900. The minimum-energy point predicts 0.756 purity, 0.900 recovery, 0.485 mol h$^{-1}$ kg$^{-1}$ productivity, and 961 kJ kgCO$_2^{-1}$ energy. The maximum-productivity point predicts 0.748 purity, 0.900 recovery, 0.504 mol h$^{-1}$ kg$^{-1}$ productivity, and 1003 kJ kgCO$_2^{-1}$ energy. The energy-productivity tradeoff is therefore milder than the purity-recovery tradeoff once purity and recovery constraints are imposed.

\section{Conclusions}
The project converts the successful gPROMS VPSA simulations into a clean scalar dataset, benchmarks tuned machine-learning surrogates, and uses the best model for multi-objective optimization. The RBF SVR gives the best Pareto-aware selection score and very high held-out accuracy across the four KPIs. NSGA-II reveals two operating regimes: a broad purity-recovery tradeoff with strong competition between objectives, and a narrower productivity-energy front under purity and recovery constraints. The workflow is reproducible through the scripts in \path{src/} and the generated CSV artifacts in \path{data/}.
